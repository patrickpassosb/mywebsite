Last weekend I participated in the **E+ Hackathon**, a competition focused on improving **PID**, the Interactive Decarbonization Platform.

The challenge was to think about how technology could help accelerate decarbonization in Brazil. Our team worked on a project called **PID — Onde Investir?**, a tool designed to help users explore investment opportunities for energy transition projects. The first use case was focused on **Roraima**, analyzing where solar energy projects could make sense, what kind of investment would be required, and how they could help reduce dependence on fossil-fuel-based energy generation.

It was a hybrid hackathon. Some people participated remotely, while others went to the on-site event. I decided to go in person.

![The beautiful, modern open-concept lounge and workspace at the E+ Hackathon venue](/articles/e-plus-hackathon/IMG-20260510-WA0015.jpeg)

At the beginning, I joined a random team without doing a deep evaluation of the people I would be working with. At first, this felt normal. In hackathons, teams are often formed quickly. You meet people, talk a little, try to understand what each person can do, and then start building.

Later, I would understand how much this decision mattered.

## Starting the Hackathon

Before the official event day, our group had already started talking about what we could build. We tried to understand the challenge, brainstorm ideas, and align on what kind of product could be useful for PID.

When I arrived at the on-site event, I met part of the team in person. Anne was there with me, and two other members were participating online. We watched the opening presentation, listened to the explanation of the challenge, and then started discussing what we were going to build.

![Participants gathered in the main auditorium during the opening presentation and challenge brief](/articles/e-plus-hackathon/IMG-20260509-WA0003.jpeg)

At that point, we were still lost.

We had some ideas, but we did not have a clear product. We did not know exactly what the user journey should be, what problem we were solving, or how our application would generate value. I felt that we needed guidance from someone who understood the context better than we did, so I went to talk with a mentor.

That conversation helped. The mentor asked questions, pushed us to clarify the idea, and helped turn a vague concept into something more concrete. After that, I could finally visualize the product better.

During the event, I also talked with Rafael Tarug, who is active in the hackathon ecosystem and the founder of the Cowworking community. We discussed competitions, technology communities, and the realities of hackathons. It was one of those conversations that happen naturally at hackathons and make the event valuable beyond the code itself.

After that, we split the work.

I would build the frontend using **Next.js, React, TypeScript, and Bun**. Another developer would build the backend using **Python and FastAPI**. Anne would help with the documentation and delivery materials. Another teammate worked on the pitch.

My role was clear: build the application.

## Building With AI Under Time Pressure

For the frontend, I used my usual workflow: AI-driven development.

I used tools like Google Stitch to quickly prototype the interface and AI coding tools to accelerate implementation. This is a workflow I have been developing for months. In hackathons, time is extremely limited, so I try to use every tool available to move faster.

With this workflow, I was able to create an initial frontend very quickly. The interface was not perfect, but it was functional, visually acceptable, and good enough to communicate the idea.

This is one thing I have learned from participating in many hackathons: judges usually do not inspect your codebase deeply. They care much more about the product, the demo, the clarity of the idea, and whether the application communicates value. Because of that, I made a trade-off. I focused less on making the code beautiful and more on making the application work and look good.

That trade-off helped me move fast, but it also created problems later.

When the backend was delivered, I reviewed it and realized that it was much simpler than I expected. It had only a few files and was not as robust as the project needed. At the same time, I was impressed that the developer had written it herself, without relying heavily on AI tools.

That was interesting to me because I have been using AI coding tools so intensely that I almost do not write code manually anymore. Seeing someone write everything directly reminded me how different people’s workflows can be.

I gave feedback as politely as I could. I said that the backend was simpler than expected and recommended tools that could help improve speed and productivity, such as Google Antigravity and uv, the Python package manager written in Rust. My intention was not to attack her work. I wanted us to move faster because we had very little time.

But the difference in workflow became a real issue.

Without AI-driven development, the backend work was moving much more slowly than I thought we could afford. Since the deadline was tight, I suggested that she focus on technical documentation while I continued building and integrating the application.

From my perspective, this was a practical decision. I thought she could contribute better in another task, while I used AI tools to move faster on the implementation.

From her perspective, it clearly did not feel that way.

## The Hardest Part Was Not the Code

At some point, I was reviewing the technical documentation and noticed a dependency listed there that did not seem to be used in the project. I asked about it.

My intention was simple: understand why that dependency was included and whether it was actually part of the application. I was trying to make the delivery more accurate.

But the question was taken personally.

The other developer reacted strongly. She said that I should not look at her work, that the documentation was not complete, and that I should focus on my own part. The conversation became tense and repetitive. I did not respond aggressively. I mostly listened.

But it drained me.

I have participated in many hackathons and competitions. I have worked with people from different countries. I have been in teams where people barely showed up. I have seen teammates disappear hours before the deadline. I have had to remove people from teams. I have been through many chaotic situations.

But this was different.

This was not just a technical problem. It was emotional exhaustion. I felt that I had asked a normal technical question and suddenly became the target of a personal reaction. That affected my energy much more than I expected.

Still, I continued building.

## Deployment Problems and Exhaustion

The application was eventually built. Because the initial backend was too simple to support our needs, I ended up having to build out the majority of the backend features myself, in addition to handling the integration. The backend was deployed on Render, but the frontend was not communicating correctly with it at first.

At one point, I had to leave the on-site location and go home. It was raining, it was cold, and the trip took around two hours. When I got home, I still had to fix the deployment.

![With my teammate Anne, taken right as we were leaving the on-site venue on the final day](/articles/e-plus-hackathon/20260510_180053.jpg)

I redeployed the application, checked previous commits, found a version that worked, and pushed it to production. Eventually, the application was live.

It worked.

It was not as good as I wanted. It never is. In every hackathon, I finish with the feeling that I could have improved the product, cleaned the code, refined the interface, fixed edge cases, and made the idea clearer. But under the constraints we had, I did what I could.

I also realized that I did not fully understand the product as deeply as I would have liked. Usually, for me to build something really strong, I need time to think about the idea, study the domain, understand the user, and let the concept mature in my mind. In this hackathon, everything moved very fast. I was building while still trying to understand what exactly we were building.

That is normal in hackathons, but it has a cost.

By the end, I had spent my tokens on Codex, used another Google account to keep working on Antigravity, pushed the application as far as I could, and exhausted almost all my energy.

I had barely eaten. I had traveled back and forth. I had handled social tension, technical pressure, deployment problems, and constant requests from the team.

Then I saw the pitch.

## The Pitch and the Final Breakdown

The pitch was below what I expected.

I had sent examples of strong pitches: well-designed, cinematic, clear, and persuasive. But the version that came back felt generic, low-resolution, and disconnected from the actual application. It looked like something generated quickly without enough context about what we had built.

At that point, I was exhausted.

The team was still asking me to change things in the application, help with tools, and provide input. But I had reached my limit. I wanted to eat, stay quiet, and recover for a moment. I ate peanuts with a banana and stopped responding for a while.

Later, when I had recovered a little, I went back to respond to the team’s messages, and they didn’t want my help anymore.

The next day, I saw a message from the other developer accusing me of being sexist, egocentric, and saying that I had acted differently because the person responsible for the pitch was not a woman.

That hit me.

I started replying, but before I could finish, she deleted the message and left the group. I then sent a message explaining my side. I said that I did not think the pitch was good, but I usually avoid giving harsh feedback publicly. My criticism was about the quality of the delivery, not about gender.

This was probably the most difficult part of the whole experience.

Before this hackathon, I had heard some men say they were afraid of working with women in competitions because a disagreement could later be framed as machismo. I used to think that this fear was exaggerated. I thought it was mostly paranoia.

After this experience, I understood why some people think that way.

I am not saying this as a general rule about women. That would be wrong. I have worked with women before and will work with women again. But this specific experience changed how I think about team formation, conflict, and personal risk in collaborative environments.

A technical disagreement can become personal. A productivity decision can be interpreted as exclusion. A criticism about delivery quality can become an accusation about character.

That is real.

## The Result

We did not place in the top three.

![Official group photo of all E+ Hackathon participants, mentors, and organizers](/articles/e-plus-hackathon/MNX06476.jpg)

When I saw the projects that won, I was not surprised. Some of them were clearly stronger. Our project had potential, but it was not refined enough. The idea could have been clearer. The pitch could have been stronger. The technical implementation could have been better. The team could have worked with more alignment.

I do not think the result was unfair.

We built something, but we did not build something strong enough to win.

## What I Learned

This hackathon reinforced something I already knew but had not felt this strongly before: choosing the right team is one of the most important parts of a hackathon.

It is not enough to have people available. It is not enough to have people who say they want to help. A team needs complementary skills, emotional stability, communication, trust, and the ability to handle pressure without turning every disagreement into a personal conflict.

![Core participant cohort posing in front of the official 'hackathon e+ \'26' backdrop](/articles/e-plus-hackathon/MNX06453.jpg)

I also learned something about myself.

I need to improve how I work in teams. I move fast, I use AI heavily, and I am very focused on execution. Sometimes that creates a gap between my rhythm and the rhythm of other people. I need to communicate better, set expectations earlier, and be more careful when redistributing tasks under pressure.

At the same time, there are situations where no communication technique can fully solve the problem. Sometimes people simply do not work well together. Sometimes the team composition is wrong. Sometimes the best lesson is to be more selective next time.

This experience changed how I will approach future hackathons.

I will still participate. I will still compete. I will still build. But I will be more careful about who I work with, how responsibilities are defined, and how conflict is handled.

Sometimes you do everything you can, and it is still not enough.

That is part of competing.

I will continue documenting these experiences because writing them down helps me understand what happened. It helps me learn from my own decisions, my mistakes, and the situations I could not control.

Hackathons are not just about building products. They also reveal how people behave under pressure.

And this one taught me that the social architecture of a team can matter as much as the technical architecture of the product.
