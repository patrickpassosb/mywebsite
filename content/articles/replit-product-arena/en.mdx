I went to the Replit Hackathon + Product Arena without a team, without a detailed plan, and without knowing exactly what I would build.

My plan was simple: arrive, listen to the ideas, find a team, and see what kind of problem was worth spending the day solving.

That is not always the most comfortable way to enter a hackathon. When you arrive without a team, there is a real risk of not finding the right people, joining a weak idea, or spending the day in a project where you cannot contribute properly. But it is also a good test. It forces you to evaluate people, problems, and opportunities quickly.

This hackathon became a useful experience for me because of that. I did not just build a project. I had to understand how to contribute inside a team that was already forming, how to deal with different working styles, and how to ship something under pressure using AI-assisted tools.

In the end, our project, **Kuatan**, placed **3rd**.

But the result was only one part of the story.

## Arriving without a team

I arrived a little late. Another participant also arrived late, and we ended up taking the elevator together. He told me he already had a teammate there, and when we reached the event floor, the initial presentations were already happening.

![The Replit Hackathon + Product Arena opening session in the Inovabra auditorium, with participants already seated](/articles/replit-product-arena/opening-presentation.jpg)

The format was simple: people presented ideas, and after that, participants could join the teams they found interesting.

There were several ideas. Some were focused on communities, some on recycling, some on beauty, some on reading, and some on healthcare. One of the ideas that caught my attention was a mental health platform.

I was considering a few options, but I kept thinking about a sentence I like:

> Entrepreneurship is caring so much about a problem that you are willing to suffer to solve it.

That helped me make the decision.

I did not care enough about some of the other ideas to spend the entire day building them. They were valid problems, but they were not the problems I personally wanted to suffer for. Mental health, healthcare, and science were more aligned with what I care about.

So I joined the mental health team.

## The idea behind Kuatan

The team was led by Daniel. The group also included Larissa, Giovanna, Marcela, and me. The team already had people with product and research skills, but it needed someone with development experience.

The idea was to build a platform to help psychologists support their patients between therapy sessions.

The core concept was that a patient could interact with an AI assistant trained around **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** principles. The assistant would not replace the psychologist. Instead, it would act as a support layer between sessions, helping the patient write notes, reflect on emotions, and maintain continuity in the therapeutic process.

For psychologists, the platform would provide summaries, emotional signals, and patient context before the next session. The goal was to help professionals understand what happened during the week, not only what the patient remembered during the appointment.

I liked the direction because it had a clear human problem and a clear product surface:

* the patient needs support between sessions;
* the psychologist needs better context;
* the AI can help organize information, but should not replace the professional;
* CBT gives the product a more structured and evidence-based foundation.

That scientific angle mattered to me. I did not want to build a vague mental health chatbot. If we were going to build something in this space, I wanted the project to respect evidence, limitations, and the role of the professional.

## From idea to prototype

After the team formed, we started discussing the product: what problem we were solving, who the users were, and what the platform should look like.

My role was mainly technical. I helped with the product direction, but my main responsibility was to help transform the idea into something visible and usable.

I started working on the design using **Google Stitch**. The goal was to create a fast visual direction that the team could agree on before we committed too much time to implementation.

This is something I have been learning across hackathons: design alignment matters. A weak interface can make a good idea look immature. A clear interface can make a prototype feel much more real, even if the backend is still limited.

Once the design direction was approved, we moved into implementation.

We used Replit and AI-assisted development. This kind of environment changes how hackathons work. You can generate interfaces, connect features, and iterate much faster than before. A small team can now build a demo in hours that would previously require much more time.

But there is also a tradeoff.

AI-assisted development makes it easier to move fast, but it does not remove the need for software engineering judgment. You still need to understand the structure of the project, the data flow, the user experience, and what is actually reliable enough to present.

This became one of the main lessons of the hackathon for me.

## Workflow friction and technical ownership

The hardest part of the day was not the idea. It was the workflow.

Daniel was highly engaged and cared deeply about the problem. That was valuable. He was pushing the project forward, making decisions, and trying to keep momentum. In a one-day hackathon, that kind of energy matters.

At the same time, we had different assumptions about how the software should be built.

I wanted to slow down slightly, align on the structure, and avoid building too many disconnected pieces. Daniel wanted to move quickly and use Replit AI to generate progress fast. Both instincts make sense in a hackathon, but they can conflict.

There was also a technical ownership issue. I was the more experienced developer in the team, but I was not fully driving the implementation from the start. That created some friction. I had to adapt to a role where I was contributing inside an existing flow instead of leading the entire technical direction.

That was uncomfortable for me.

In many projects and hackathons, I naturally take a leadership role. I organize the technical work, define the architecture, unblock people, and push the project forward. In this case, I had to operate differently. I had to contribute without trying to restart everything from zero.

That is a real skill.

Sometimes being useful is not about controlling the project. Sometimes it is about understanding the current direction, identifying where you can add value, and improving the result without creating unnecessary conflict.

I worked on features in a separate GitHub branch and tried to avoid breaking the main project. When the feature was ready, I explained what I had done and suggested testing it before merging. That workflow helped reduce risk.

I also learned that I need to improve how I explain technical work to non-technical teammates. At one point, other team members asked what I had built, and I explained it in a way that was too technical. They did not fully understand it.

That was my mistake.

In a hackathon, the technical work only creates value if the team understands how it supports the pitch, the demo, and the story.

## Building with Replit AI and vibe coding

This hackathon also made me think more about vibe coding.

Replit AI and similar tools are powerful. They let people build faster, especially in environments where the goal is to create a working prototype in a short period of time.

For hackathons, that is a major advantage.

You can generate components, test ideas, create screens, and move from concept to demo very quickly. The barrier to building is lower, and people who are not traditional developers can participate more actively in software creation.

But there is a risk: people can confuse a generated prototype with a well-built product.

A demo can look functional while still being fragile. A screen can look complete while the logic behind it is shallow. A feature can appear finished while the architecture is difficult to maintain.

This does not mean vibe coding is bad. It means the role of the developer is changing.

The developer is no longer only the person who writes every line of code manually. In this kind of workflow, the developer also needs to be the person who evaluates, directs, fixes, structures, and integrates what AI generates.

The value is not just typing code.

The value is judgment.

## The pitch

When the deadline approached, the team shifted focus to the presentation. The pitch needed to explain the problem, the users, the product, and the impact clearly.

Our project had a strong emotional angle because mental health is a problem that many people understand personally. That matters in a public-vote environment. The most technical project does not always win. The project that people remember, understand, and emotionally connect with often has an advantage.

Kuatan had a good story:

* mental health support between therapy sessions;
* AI as an assistant, not a replacement;
* CBT as a structured foundation;
* useful summaries for psychologists;
* a simple product experience for patients.

The pitch went well. Daniel presented the project, and the rest of us supported from the side.

After our presentation, we watched the remaining pitches. There were many different projects, including applications for fitness, reading, entertainment, beauty, accessibility, recycling, and health.

Some projects were technically interesting. Some had better stories. Some were more polished. That is one of the useful parts of a hackathon: you get to see how different teams interpret the same constraints.

## The result: 3rd place

After the pitches, the judging and voting process started.

I felt we had a realistic chance of placing in the top three. Our project was not necessarily the most technically complex, but it was relevant, understandable, and emotionally strong. The UI was good enough, the pitch was clear, and the problem mattered.

When the organizers announced the results, **Kuatan placed 3rd**.

![The Kuatan team standing in front of the event screen showing '3º lugar: Kuatan' — the 3rd place result](/articles/replit-product-arena/result-third-place.jpg)

I wanted first place, so part of me was disappointed. That is normal. I enter competitions because I want to win.

But placing 3rd was still a good result, especially considering that I arrived without a team and joined the project on the same day. We also won **200 Replit credits** and **one day at the inovabra coworking space**.

More importantly, I left with a better understanding of how I work inside teams and what I want from future hackathons.

## What I learned

The biggest lesson was that team motivation matters.

The people on the team cared about the problem. That made a difference. Even with workflow issues, the team had a shared reason to keep working. People were not just building something random. They had personal or professional reasons to care about mental health.

That is something I want to prioritize more in future competitions.

A strong team is not just a group of people with skills. It is a group of people who care enough about the same problem to keep pushing when the work becomes uncomfortable.

The second lesson was about role adaptation.

I am used to being one of the people driving the project. In this hackathon, I had to learn how to contribute without fully controlling the direction. That was not easy, but it was useful.

The third lesson was about communication.

Technical work needs translation. If I build something but cannot explain why it matters to the pitch, the user, or the business, then I am not creating as much value as I could.

The fourth lesson was about AI-assisted development.

Tools like Replit AI are changing hackathons. They make building faster, but they also make technical judgment more important. The future is not only about who can code. It is about who can guide machines, evaluate outputs, and turn generated software into something coherent.

## Becoming more selective

I still think hackathons are valuable.

They teach execution, teamwork, product thinking, pitching, and resilience. They force you to build under pressure. They expose you to people, ideas, and constraints that you would not find by studying alone.

But I am also becoming more selective.

Hackathons have an opportunity cost. I spend time, energy, transportation, and focus. If the prize is small, the network is weak, or the opportunity is not aligned with my goals, I need to think carefully before participating.

That does not mean I will only join hackathons with large prizes. Some small events are worth it because of the people, the theme, or the learning opportunity.

But going forward, I want to choose better.

I want to participate in hackathons where I can build something valuable, meet strong people, improve my skills, and compete for meaningful opportunities.

The Replit Hackathon + Product Arena was a good experience. I built with a new team, shipped under pressure, practiced working with AI-assisted development, and placed 3rd with Kuatan.

It was not perfect.

That is why it was useful.
